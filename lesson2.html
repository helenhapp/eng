Past Simple Tense

Past Simple

We use the Past Simple tense to talk about actions that started and finished in the past. It is the most common way to describe past events.

  • Recent or distant past: It doesn't matter if the action happened a second ago or a hundred years ago.
    • I saved my file a minute ago.
    • Ada Lovelace wrote the first algorithm in 1843.
  • With or without time expressions: We often use time words like yesterday, last week, two days ago, or in 2023. However, sometimes the time is simply understood from the context.
    • I debugged the code yesterday.
    • The computer crashed!
  • One or many actions: We use it for a single completed action, a repeated action in the past, or a sequence of actions.
    • I clicked the button.
    • I practiced Python every day last summer.
    • I opened the laptop, wrote some code, and ran the program.

Regular and Irregular Verbs

To make sentences in the Past Simple, we need to change the verb into its past form (also called V2). There are two types of verbs in English: Regular and Irregular.

Regular Verbs

For most regular verbs we just add -ed in the end. But there are some special spelling rules you need to remember:

  1. Verbs ending in -e: If the verb already ends in an -e, just add -d.
    • save → saved
    • delete → deleted
  2. Verbs ending in a consonant + y: If the verb ends in a consonant and a -y, change the y to i and add -ed.
    • study → studied
    • try → tried
    Careful! If there is a vowel before the y (a, e, i, o, u), just add -ed normally:
    • play → played
  3. The C-V-C Rule (Consonant-Vowel-Consonant): If a short word ends in a Consonant, a Vowel, and a Consonant, we double the last letter before adding -ed.
    • stop → stopped
    • plan → planned
    • debug → debugged

Positive and Negative Sentences

Positive Sentences

Formula: Subject + Verb (Past form)

  • I found the bug.
  • She ran the code.

Negative Sentences

Formula: Subject + didn't + Verb (Base form)

  • ✅ Correct: I did not find the bug.
  • ✅ Correct: I didn't find the bug.
  • ❌ Incorrect: I didn't found the bug.
  • ❌ Incorrect: I not found the bug.

Questions in Past Simple Tense

Questions

In life, we ask questions to get information. In programming, asking the system for data is called a query. When we want to query the past in English, we use specific formulas to build our questions correctly.

Question Types

There are three main types of questions in the Past Simple tense. The difference between them depends on what information we are looking for: a simple confirmation (True/False), a specific detail, or the "doer" of the action.

Type 1: General (Yes/No) Questions

General questions are used to check if an action happened, and they only return a Yes or No answer.

Formula: Did + subject + base verb + ...?

  • Did the program run correctly?
  • Did you import the math library?
  • Did the turtle draw a triangle?

Short Answers

When answering a Yes/No question, we do not repeat the main verb. We just use the helper verb "did" or "didn't".

  • ✅ Yes, it did.
  • ✅ No, it didn't.
  • ❌ Yes, it did run.
  • ❌ No, it didn't work.

⚡ Pro Tip (Emphatic "Did"): There is one exception! You can use did + base verb in a positive sentence if you want to strongly emphasize that something really happened (usually to prove someone wrong).

  • — You didn't save the file!
  • — Yes, I did save it!

Type 2: Special Questions (Wh-)

We use Special Questions to ask about a specific detail: place, time, reason, or method. These questions always start with a special question word (Wh-word).

Formula: Wh-word + did + subject + base verb + ...?

Wh- Word Translation Example
What що / який What did you import?
Where де / куди Where did you save the file?
When коли When did the error happen?
Why чому Why did the program stop?
How як How did you solve the equation?

Type 3: Subject Questions (Who / What)

This is the trickiest type! Let's break down why it is special.

In a regular question, did stands between the question word and the subject (між питальним словом та підметом):

  • What did you break?
  • What = question word
  • you = subject

But sometimes, Who or What IS the subject. We are asking about the person or thing that performed the action. In this case, there is no separate subject, so there is nowhere to put "did". So the word "did" disappears, and the verb stays in the past tense !

Formula: Who / What + past verb + ...? (Without did!)

What we know Question Why?
You broke something. What was it? What did you break? "You" is the subject, so "did" is in its place.
Something broke your code. What was it? What broke your code? "What" is the subject, so we don't use "did".
She wrote something. What was it? What did she write? "She" is the subject, so "did" is in its place.
Someone wrote this program. Who was it? Who wrote this program? "Who" is the subject, so we don't use "did".

A few more examples without "did":

  • Who deleted my file? (Not: Who did delete)
  • What caused the syntax error? (Not: What did cause)
  • What broke the server?
